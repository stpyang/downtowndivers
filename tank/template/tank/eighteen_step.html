PSI-PCI 18-step protocol

  1. SLOWLY Drain cylinder
    • Slowly discharge all air from cylinder with the reserve (if any) down.
    • Avoid heavy condensation on the cylinder interior walls.
    • Should take longer than 10 minutes.
  2. Observe & Record Background Data
    • Record relevant cylinder background data as indicated on the evaluation form.
    • Determine if the cylinder is within hydrostatic test date and acceptable to inspect.
      • If hydro test is not available in your country or region, the visual inspection becomes even more vital.
    • Look for evidence of heat damage
    • Learn method used for any repainting observed.
    • Inquire of owner for any information about the cylinder that might influence the inspection.
  3. Remove All Accessories
    • Remove all accessories including backpack, cover, boot and attachments.
    • Remove loose paint and dirt from exterior.
    • Expose all the tank printed specifications relevant to the inspection.
  4. Administer Hammer Test to Steel Cylinder
    • Perform hammer test for steel cylinders only.
    • Hold bare cylinder off the floor by the valve and strike lightly with a mallet.
    • Assess tone for bell-like quality
    • The hamer test may release corrosion product adhering to interior walls.
  5. Exterior Inspection
    • Place cylinder on a padded bench and roll it 360 degrees with a straight edge.
      • For 3AA and 3AL cylinder only, dress protusion gouges with a file to assess true depth.
    • Assess depth observations against the allowable limits.
    • Assess composite damage against limits found in CGA6.2 tables or manufacturer's limits.
  6. Remove Valve
    • Carefully open valve to verify that the cylinder is empty.
    • Secure cylidner in padded restraint (vise).
    • Un-padded restraint may mar cylinder finish or damage metal and composite materials.
    • Remove valve
      • The valve may be difficult to remove.
      • Impatience and improper use of tools can cause damage to the valve.
      • Remember which way the valve unscrews.
      • If release is difficult, consider the possibility of air remaining behind a defective valve.
      • Anticipate o-ring extrusion.
      • The heel of your hand may provide enough force to release a 3/4" straight thread valve.
      • Do not strike valve handwheel or reserve mechanism repeatedly to loosen.
      • Place wrench across valve flats near cylinder neck or use distributor provided tool.
      • Use care to avoid deforming valve.
      • For hard to remove valves, add a small amount of air into cylinder and then release it to confirm the cylinder is empty.
      • It may be necessary to carefully remove the burst disc if valve will not release air.
      • Some valves have a pressure release hole in the threaded area to alert the technician that air still remains in the cylinder.
    • Inspect the valve for proper function of the handwheel and the reserve (if present).
    • Inspect the threads, base and dip tube for evidence of corrosion or contaminents.
    • Have valve serviced or cleaned if defective or dirty.
    • Replace plastic dip tubes found on older US Divers and Scuba Pro valves.
  7. Check for and Remove Foreign Objects
    • Insert inspection light part way into the cylinder, look briefly to determine the presence of water or solids in the cylinder.
    • If extraordinary contents are present, release the cylinder from the vise and carefully the contents into a suitable container.
    • When no significant contents are present, release cylinder from vise and invert cylinder about 1/2" above a white piece of paper placed on a padded surface. Then allow the cylinder to drop onto the paper, thereby releasing its nominal contents.
    • Assess the contents, record the type and amount (retain contents in plastic bag to show the owner).
      • Aluminum:
        • White powder, usually is aluminum oxide.
        • Gummy, translucent lumps is aluminum hydroxide.
      • Steel:
        • Brown powder, grit and flakes usually are rust.
        • Black dust may be compressor-filter carbon.
  8. View/Inspect Interior of Cylinder
    • With multi-bulb low voltage inspection light, carefully view and assess all visible parts of the cylinder interior. Determine the source of any contents.
    • Use pick-end probe tools to compare suspect pits and corrosion areas against the standard pit reference plate and record the findings using descriptive terms.
    • If needed, the cylinder should be tumbled by an qualified person during the inspection process to remove any material obscuring the metal surface.
    • After tumbling the inspection continues.
    • Inspectors without such equipment should arrange for tumbling services.
  9. Inspect Cylinder Threads
    • Carefully view all cylinder threads. Ensure that a minimum of full and continuous threads are present.
    • Some aluminum alloy cylinders are susceptible to extensive metal loss at the base of the threads.
    • Galling from valve removal and galvanic corrosion may reduce threads on aluminum alloy cylinders and composite linders.
    • Consult manufacturer for the necessary number of intact threads.
    • Use a shoulder mirror to inspect the shoulder and neck area of aluminum cylinders for stress cracks or other damage.
  10. Eddy Current
    • Applicable 3AL cylinders, all scuba, SCBA and oxygen cylinders manufactured prior to 1990.
  11. Inspect the O-ring
    • Commonly the valve o-ring can be used again if it is not damaged. However, some valve distributors and conservative inspectors routinely replace the valve o-ring.
    • Selection for proper material compounds is important. Use manufacturer's recommend compound and hardness.
  12. Inspect the Pressure Relief Device (PRD)
    • Ensure correct pressure rating for cylinder.
    • If the PRD assembly is lead filled, it should be replaced with correct safety device.
    • If the retainer nut is single port it should be replaced with a multi-port nut assembly.
    • The multi-port retaining nuts should be used for replacements as they prevent the cylinder moving if disc ruptures.
    • Valve manufacturers recommend entire disc assembly on SCUBA cylinders be replaced as a unit each 5-years when no other justification warrants more frequent services. Use manufacturers' specified interval for SCBA cylinder valves.
  13. Make Your Judgement.
    • The judgment is made after all the observations are complete.
    • If the cylinder meets the standards, then re-install the valve.
    • If the cylinder does not pass the inspection, tag the cylinder with an explanatory note describing the owner's options (see evaluation form).
    • Contact the owner and decide if the cylinder should be re-assembled or sent to (1) an hydro test station, (2) tumbling, (3) other service, or (4) condemned.
  14. Reinstall Valve
    • For all AIR cylinders with straight thread o-ring sealed valves, apply light coat of Dow Corning Compound 111 (or equivalent) to all threads. This is an essential step for aluminum cylinder to prevent galvanic corrosion.
    • Do not overly tighten o-ring sealed valves.
    • Tapered 1/2" pipe threads should be covered by 2 wraps of Teflon tape (medic oxygen cylinders).
      • Tape wrap should not extend beyond valve threads since excess tape can become detached from the valve and enter the breathing supply, possibly fouling the valve or regulator.
    • Set valve using only modest hand force on the wrench, approximately 45 to 75 foot pounds of torque.
    • For multi-tank assemblies, leave the manifold only hand tight until backpack or bands are in place.
    • Then be sure all is square and parallel as the manifold is tightened snugly.
  15. Complete Assembly and Apply Evidence of Inspection (EOI) Sticker
    • If the cylinder meets the standards, log the inspection report into a permanent record or apply an EOI sticker with the current date. Use a paper hole punch to obliterate the inspection month and year when PSI EOI are used.
    • Reinstall all accessories such as backpack and cylinder cover.
  16. Fill Cylinder and Check for Leaks
    • Inspectors wth an air station typically refill the cylinder and inspect for leaks (gas compatible solution). However, the inspection is complete without re-filling cylinder with air.
    • Check the valve pressure guage for accuracy.
  17. Complete All Forms and Paperwork For Each Cylinder
  18. Return Cylinder to Owner
    • Return cylinder to owner with a list of tips for cylinder care and storage (see back of evaluation form).
    • Return all parts replaced for the owner's inspection.
    • Provide the owner with a copy of the written report.
